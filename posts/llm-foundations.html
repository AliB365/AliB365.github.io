How Large Language Models Work

Tokens, Not Words

Every interaction with an LLM begins with tokenization. Your prompt is split into smaller pieces called tokens, "Hello, World!" becomes ["Hello", ",", "World", "!"]. Tokens are how models understand structure. They can represent entire words, subwords, punctuation, or even whitespace.

Once tokenized, the model predicts the most likely next token based on everything it has seen. The output you read is assembled token by token, which is why some responses visibly "stream" in real time.

Training Data and Patterns

Modern LLMs are trained on massive datasets that blend web pages, books, articles, documentation, and code. They don't memorize all of that content; instead, they learn statistical patterns about how ideas connect. This pattern matching lets models form coherent paragraphs, switch writing styles, and solve problems they've never seen verbatim.

What LLMs Do Well

  • Conversational interfaces: Personal assistants, support chatbots, and brainstorming companions
  • Content generation: Articles, emails, creative briefs, marketing copy, and social posts
  • Code assistance: Writing, refactoring, and explaining units of code across languages
  • Summaries and translation: Distilling long documents and moving between languages quickly
  • Knowledge retrieval: Answering questions from large knowledge bases or notes

Limits You Need to Respect

Knowing the guardrails helps you avoid frustration:

  • Hallucinations: LLMs can invent confident but incorrect answers when data is sparse
  • Training cutoff: Each model only knows events up to a certain date
  • Bias: Models mirror the gaps and biases present in their training sets
  • Context windows: Exceeding the maximum token count will cause earlier information to drop
  • No true understanding: Models pattern-match, they don't possess lived experience or intent
